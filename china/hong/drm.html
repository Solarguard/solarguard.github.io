A Dream of Red Mansions (Hung Lou Meng)

Tsao Hsueh-chin and Kao Ngo, transl. Yang Hsien-yi and Gladys Yang, Foreign Languages Press, Peking, 1978.

Some consider this the best Chinese novel. It was written about 250 years ago and thus may be seen as a culmination of novels from the Ming Dynasty (1368-1644). Since the reading and writing of novels was not considered proper in imperial China, the authorship of this one has been attributed, according to Twentieth Century scholarship, to Ts'ao Hsueh-Ch'in and Kao Ou.

This is a long (close to 1,900 pages) family story. Even if the authors of novels in imperial China withheld their names from such frivolous work, this reader was quite impressed by the highly polished quality of the account of the Chia family. Largely, what we see is the young aristocrat Pao-yu and his relatives and maids who through much of the story are living in the mansion's exquisite garden.

Pao-yu is supposed to be preparing himself for the state exam by which one entered the civil service. At one point his father tells him to present himself the next morning with the work he has been doing for the past three years. That night, Pao-yu's maids stay up writing out calligraphy assignments in Pao-yu's handwriting. Pao-yu is able to impress his father, however, even before being called on to produce such proof of his ongoing studies. He does this by composing a poem.

Poetry is very evident in this work. The characters, at least Pao-yu and other dwellers of the Garden, often compose poems. They even form a poetry club, and, I believe, it is at the first meeting that they compose poems on the theme of chrysanthemums. This is described fully and a dozen poems occur at this point of the text. There is rich allusion to China's history and culture. For example, at one point a character writes a poem which includes the phrase, "Tapping at the door." There is a note here by the translators that a famous T'ang poet (hence about a thousand years before this novel was written) had deliberated on the use of, "Tapping," or, "Pushing," here, and this came to be used to indicate careful composition.

The characters are well drawn (I especially like the Old Lady), the window provided by the book on many aspects of imperial China fascinating, and the way the plot proceeds does not appeal to me. It is still a great piece of literature and I read to the end.
